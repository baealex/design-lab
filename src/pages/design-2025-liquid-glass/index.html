Inspired by Apple Liquid Glass

Liquid
Glass

A translucent functional layer above content, shaped by optical depth and able to transform as its context changes.

Context

Apple introduced Liquid Glass across its platforms in 2025. It moved controls into a shared material above content: edges catch light, underlying color appears refracted, and controls can change shape between states. The useful idea is not transparency everywhere. It is a consistent functional layer that keeps the content underneath in focus.

Principle 01 · Optical material

Refraction describes depth

A molded control is not equally distorted from edge to edge. Its curved rim bends the scene strongly; the flatter center settles into mild magnification so content stays readable. Change the edge intensity below—the center remains at one fifth of that value.

Principle 02 · Floating controls

Actions sit above the work

Buttons share one compact floating surface, while the chosen action expands just enough to expose its label. Each button below changes the document state; none is ornamental.

Principle 04 · Adaptive contrast

Legibility follows the scene

Our take

What we keep / What we improve

Keep

  • Keep controls in a coherent functional layer above the content.
  • Use refraction, highlights, and shape changes to explain depth and state.
  • Let toolbars consolidate around the current task instead of adding more chrome.

Improve

  • Guarantee contrast and readability; transparency must never be the only treatment.
  • Limit transparency to controls, not every section or content surface.
  • Use motion only to explain state changes and respect reduced-motion preferences.
  • Cap distortion, blur fallback, and layer count, with an opaque fallback for performance and support.
Liquid Glass is most useful as interface architecture: a responsive control layer, not a page-wide visual effect.

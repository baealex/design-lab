Inspired by Android Lollipop

Material

Paper, ink, and motion gave Android interfaces a shared spatial logic.
We keep the spatial logic. Free it from the template.

Material Design gave motion meaning. The ripple tells you "I heard you, right here." The shadow tells you "I'm above that." The easing curve tells you "I came from there." Material formalized physics as a shared design language.

The problem was rigidity. Unrelated apps risked resembling Google products. Prescriptions such as "cards at 2dp, dialogs at 24dp, FAB at bottom-right" were often copied as a template instead of adapted as a toolkit.

We keep the physics. We drop the dogma. Elevation as hierarchy, ripple as honest feedback, bold color as identity — these are principles worth preserving. A fixed component recipe is not.

Principle 01

Paper Elevation

Every surface is a sheet of paper at a specific height. The shadow isn't a style choice — it's a z-coordinate. Google's guidance mapped common surfaces to named elevation levels. We do not copy those values as a fixed recipe, but we keep the rule: higher means more important. Hover these cards — they rise, and their shadow tells you they did.

Principle 02

Ink Ripple

A ripple ties touch feedback to the point of contact. It radiates from the exact point of contact — not a generic highlight, not a color swap. A physics-based wave that confirms "yes, here, I felt that." On light surfaces the ink is dark. On colored surfaces it's light. We keep this. Every interactive surface on this page ripples from where you actually clicked.

Principle 03

Meaningful Motion

Nothing teleports. Elements expand from their origin, transform into their destination. The easing curve is cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1) — fast in, slow out. Google called it "standard easing." It answers "where did this come from?" If the animation can't answer that question, kill it. Click a chip to see it expand from where it was.

Palette

Bold Color

Material shipped a palette of named colors at fixed saturation stops — 500 for primary, 700 for the status bar, A200 for accents. A strong app-bar color became a recognizable Material pattern. For this Lollipop study, one saturated primary carries the app bar, active controls, and touch feedback without becoming a universal rule.

Components

Raised, Flat, FAB

Three button types, each with a clear role. Raised buttons cast shadow — they're for primary actions that need to stand out. Flat buttons sit flush — they're for secondary actions inside cards or dialogs. The FAB floats above content when a screen has one recurring primary action. We keep that hierarchy without forcing a FAB everywhere. The uppercase is Lollipop's voice. The ripple on click is its handshake.

All Together

Lollipop Inbox

Status bar at 700. App bar at 500. Cards with 2dp shadow. A FAB for compose. Ripples on interactive rows. The original Material formula — paper, ink, bold color, physics — assembled into a recognizable 2014 Android pattern. Google apps and many apps following the defaults adopted variations of it. We show the pattern here to study its logic, not to prescribe a template.

What This Is Not

  • ✗ Treating the guidance as a fixed template — adapt principles to the product
  • ✗ Every app looking like Gmail — your app has its own identity
  • ✗ FAB on every screen — not everything needs a primary action
  • ✗ Ripple on static text — ink responds to interaction only
  • ✓ Elevation as spatial hierarchy — higher means louder
  • ✓ Ink ripple at the exact point of touch — honest feedback
  • ✓ Bold primary color as identity — pick one, commit
  • ✓ Motion that explains origin — no teleporting, no mystery

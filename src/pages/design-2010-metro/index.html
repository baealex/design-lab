Inspired by Windows Phone 7

Metro

Metro put content before chrome.
We keep the clarity and restore the cues each input method needs.

Metro emerged with Windows Phone 7 in 2010, when smartphone interfaces were crowded with glossy icons, physical metaphors, and app-by-app chrome. Drawing on transit signage, it used large type, flat color, panoramic layouts, and live tiles to make information easy to scan on a small touch screen.

The same language exposed different weaknesses when Microsoft carried it into Windows 8 in 2012. Hidden navigation and touch-first gestures reached mouse-and-keyboard users without enough visible cues. The lesson is not that flatness failed; a visual language has to adapt its affordances to the device.

We preserve the content-first philosophy while making sure users can find and operate things. Every tile on this page is glanceable, every section is discoverable, and the typography does the navigation for you.

Principle 01

Typography IS the Interface

Metro's central claim: you don't always need icons, borders, or boxes. A 42px light-weight header says "this is important." A 12px uppercase label says "this is a category." The type hierarchy does what buttons and panels used to do. The hierarchy can carry much of that work on its own.

Principle 02

The Living Tile

Not an icon. Not a button. A tile — a content-forward rectangle that updates and informs you without ever opening the app. Your email count, the weather, your next meeting — all glanceable, all alive. The home screen wasn't a launcher. It was a dashboard. It turned a launcher into an at-a-glance information surface.

Principle 03

Chromeless Design

Remove the border. Remove the shadow. Remove the rounded corner. Now remove the background. What's left? The content. That's Metro's gambit: content is the chrome. But Metro went too far — removing all affordance made users guess. Our take: keep the chromeless purity, but let the interface respond to attention. Hover the right column below.

What This Is Not

Metro's strongest ideas came from a touch-first phone, while its weakest moments appeared when those ideas moved to other inputs without adaptation. Keep the hierarchy and live information; do not hide essential navigation or interaction cues.

  • ✗ Removing ALL visual affordance — users must know what's interactive
  • ✗ Forcing touch UI on mouse and keyboard users
  • ✗ Flat to the point of confusion — if everything looks the same, nothing communicates
  • ✗ Tiles without meaningful content — a colored box with just an icon is a bad icon
  • ✓ Let typography create hierarchy — size and weight replace borders and boxes
  • ✓ Use color blocks as navigation — bold, confident, unmistakable
  • ✓ Content-first, chrome-never — if it's not content, it shouldn't be there
  • ✓ Bold but not confusing — clarity is not the enemy of minimalism

Let content lead.
Let type organize.
Let affordance fit the device.

Windows Phone 7 showed how little chrome a clear touch interface needed. Windows 8 showed why the same answer cannot be copied across every device unchanged.

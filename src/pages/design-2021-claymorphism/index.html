Inspired by Claymorphism

Clay

Soft 3D surfaces moved from illustration into interface design.
The style works when form, contrast, and context agree.

Claymorphism emerged around 2021 as soft 3D illustration became common in product branding and interface galleries. It combined neumorphism's shadow-built depth with pastel surfaces, large radii, and an inflated clay-like form.

Many reference images were rendered in 3D tools; CSS versions are a flatter approximation built from outer and inset shadows. That distinction matters: decoration can suggest material, but controls still need labels, contrast, and recognizable states.

The following three principles separate the style's form, palette, and appropriate use. Clay is a visual voice, not a complete interaction model.

Principle 01 · Form

Soft Volume

A three-shadow recipe creates the form. An outer shadow lifts the element off the page. An inner dark shadow on the bottom-right simulates the clay curving away from the light. An inner light shadow on the top-left catches the highlight. Together they create the illusion of a soft, inflated 3D shape — using nothing but box-shadow and border-radius.

Principle 02 · Color

Color With Contrast

The shadow formula is related to neumorphism, but colored surfaces make cards and groups easier to distinguish. Color supports hierarchy; it does not replace readable text or explicit control states.

Principle 03 · Context

Context Earns the Style

Clay's rounded, illustrative character can support products such as children's learning, pet care, or a playful onboarding experience. Dense financial, legal, or operational tools may need a quieter surface treatment so information remains primary.

Treat claymorphism as a personality layer, not a universal system. Apply it to selected cards, illustrations, or key actions and keep content-heavy areas straightforward.

Working Example

The Rules

  • ✗ Clay everything — not every product has a playful personality
  • ✗ Pretending CSS clay equals Blender clay — those viral shots are 3D renders
  • ✗ Heavy clay effects on dense or critical data — prioritize scanability
  • ✗ Triple shadows on every element — the GPU budget is real
  • ✗ Elements too close together — overlapping shadows break the clay illusion and look floaty
  • ✓ Clay in selected playful or illustrative contexts
  • ✓ A limited pastel palette with tested foreground contrast
  • ✓ Small elements first — buttons, badges, cards. Not full-page layouts
  • ✓ Outer and inset shadows used consistently from one light direction
